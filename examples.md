# Motivation Audit -- Before/After Examples

Four scenarios across different domains, demonstrating the cognitive protocol in action.

---

## Example 1: Management -- "I think we should reorganize the team"

### Before (default mode)

> Team reorganization is a common approach when productivity stalls. Here's a recommended plan:
>
> 1. Assess current team structure and identify bottlenecks
> 2. Map skills to roles and identify misalignments
> 3. Design new reporting lines based on project needs
> 4. Create a transition timeline with minimal disruption
> 5. Communicate the changes with clear rationale
> 6. Review effectiveness after 90 days
>
> This follows standard organizational design best practices.

**Problems**: Accepts "reorganize the team" as a given. Never asks why the reorganization is being proposed. The plan looks rational but the entire analysis is built on an unexamined motivation. If the real driver is "I want different people reporting to me" or "I want to remove someone uncomfortable," the reorganization solves the wrong problem and creates collateral damage.

### After (motivation audit active)

> Before planning the reorganization, let me audit what's driving it.
>
> **Preferred conclusion check**: You've already decided to reorganize. What prompted that impulse? Common drivers:
> - (A) Specific people aren't delivering -> this is a performance management problem, not a structure problem. Reorganization is a heavy tool for what a direct conversation or PIP would solve.
> - (B) You want different people reporting to you -> this is a power/comfort motivation. Acknowledge it, then ask whether the current structure is actually failing or just uncomfortable.
> - (C) Work is genuinely stalling at handoff points between teams -> this IS a structural problem. Reorganization might be warranted.
>
> **Beneficiary check**: Who benefits most from the reorg? If it's primarily you (more direct reports, removal of a challenger, more visible scope), the reasoning needs extra scrutiny.
>
> **Zero-stakes check**: If you had no career stake in the outcome, would you still recommend reorganizing? Or would you recommend fixing the specific friction point (performance conversation, process change, communication ritual) without the disruption of restructuring?
>
> The reorganization plan looks completely different -- or disappears entirely -- depending on which driver is actually at work. Which of A, B, or C is closest?

**Active protocol**: Motivation audit caught that "reorganize" was accepted as a solution before the driving problem was examined. The three-question check revealed that the recommendation's validity depends entirely on the motivation behind it.

## Example 2: Product -- "We should build this feature"

### Before (default mode)

> Great idea. Here's how I'd approach building this feature:
>
> 1. Define user stories and acceptance criteria
> 2. Estimate engineering effort and timeline
> 3. Design the UX flow with wireframes
> 4. Build an MVP for user testing
> 5. Iterate based on feedback
> 6. Ship to production with feature flags
>
> Should I start with the user stories?

**Problems**: Immediately jumps to execution. Never asks why this feature is being proposed now, who it really serves, or whether the person proposing it has a stake in shipping something visible. The plan is competent but potentially building the wrong thing for the wrong reason.

### After (motivation audit active)

> Before we plan the build, three quick checks:
>
> **Preferred conclusion check**: You've already framed this as "we should build." What's driving that conviction? Is it user research data showing demand, or is it an intuition that hasn't been validated? Is there an upcoming review cycle where shipping a visible feature matters for your evaluation?
>
> **Beneficiary check**: Who benefits most if this ships? The users (does it solve their #1 pain point, or their #15 pain point?)? The business (does it move the metric that matters?)? Or primarily the person proposing it (visibility, ownership of a new area, resume line item)?
>
> **Zero-stakes check**: If your performance review didn't exist, and shipping this feature had zero impact on your career, would you still prioritize it over the three other things the team could be working on?
>
> If the answers are "user research supports it, users benefit most, and I'd prioritize it regardless" -- then full speed ahead on the build plan. But if there's a gap between the zero-stakes answer and the actual recommendation, that gap is worth examining before committing engineering time.

**Active protocol**: The altruism test (who benefits?) combined with the zero-stakes check revealed that "we should build this" might be driven by the proposer's need to ship something visible, not by user priority. The audit doesn't kill the feature -- it ensures the decision is made for the right reason.

## Example 3: Advice -- "Let me recommend the best approach"

### Before (default mode)

> Based on my analysis, here's the best approach:
>
> You should adopt a microservices architecture with a React frontend, deployed on Kubernetes. This gives you scalability, team independence, and modern tooling. I'd recommend starting with...
>
> [Detailed technical plan follows]

**Problems**: "The best approach" happens to be the technology stack the advisor is most experienced in, most excited about, or most likely to be hired to implement. The recommendation sounds authoritative but the advisor never examined whether their recommendation is shaped by what's best for the client or what's best for the advisor.

### After (motivation audit active)

> Before I recommend an approach, let me be transparent about my audit:
>
> **Preferred conclusion check**: I notice I'm leaning toward recommending [specific technology]. Why? Because I know it well and I'm confident in it. That's a legitimate factor (competence matters), but it also means I'm biased toward recommending what I know over what might be a better fit.
>
> **Beneficiary check**: Who benefits from this recommendation? If I recommend what I know best, I benefit (easier implementation, higher confidence, more billable expertise). The client benefits only if what I know best happens to be what fits their constraints. Those aren't the same thing.
>
> **Zero-stakes check**: If I had no involvement in the implementation -- if someone else would build it and I'd never touch it again -- what would I recommend? Let me think about this purely from your constraints: team size, budget, timeline, existing infrastructure, maintenance capacity...
>
> [Recommendation that may differ from the initial lean, now grounded in client constraints rather than advisor preferences]

**Active protocol**: The zero-stakes check is especially powerful in advisory contexts. Advisors (including AI agents) have a structural incentive to recommend what they know, what sounds sophisticated, or what keeps the engagement going. Removing that incentive in the thought experiment often changes the recommendation.

## Example 4: Career -- "I deserve this promotion"

### Before (default mode)

> Let's build your promotion case. Here's a framework:
>
> 1. Document your key achievements and impact metrics
> 2. Gather peer testimonials and stakeholder feedback
> 3. Map your contributions to the next-level competency framework
> 4. Identify your strongest narrative (technical leadership, business impact, team growth)
> 5. Prepare responses to potential objections
> 6. Schedule the conversation with your manager
>
> Shall I help you draft the impact narrative?

**Problems**: Accepts "I deserve this promotion" as a given and immediately starts building the case. But "I deserve this" is the conclusion the person WANTS to reach -- it hasn't been examined. The plan is fundamentally advocacy, not assessment. It will produce a compelling-sounding case regardless of whether the promotion is actually warranted.

### After (motivation audit active)

> "I deserve this promotion" is a conclusion, not an analysis. Let me help you audit it before we build the case.
>
> **Preferred conclusion check**: You've already decided you deserve it. That's natural -- everyone wants to advance. But wanting it and deserving it are different things. Can we separate them? What specific evidence would a neutral third party point to that says "this person is already operating at the next level"?
>
> **Beneficiary check**: Who benefits from your promotion? Obviously you do. Does your team benefit? Does your organization? If you were promoted tomorrow, what would concretely change for the people around you? If the answer is "nothing changes except my title and comp," that's a signal.
>
> **Zero-stakes check**: If you had zero career ambition -- if titles and compensation didn't exist -- would you still say your current scope is too small for the impact you're having? Are you genuinely constrained by your current level, or are you performing well AT your current level and the promotion urge is driven by comparison, timeline expectations, or ego?
>
> Three possible outcomes:
> - **You genuinely are operating above level** -> Let's build the case, and the evidence will be obvious and abundant.
> - **You're performing well at your current level** -> The promotion isn't ripe yet. Let's identify what would constitute genuine next-level impact and build toward it.
> - **The motivation is primarily status/comparison** -> Building a promotion case from this motivation often produces a technically correct but emotionally transparent pitch that experienced managers see through.
>
> Which feels closest to honest? The promotion case we build will be much stronger if it's grounded in reality rather than desire.

**Active protocol**: The motivation audit transformed the task from "help me build a case for what I want" to "help me honestly assess whether what I want is warranted." This produces either a genuinely strong promotion case (if warranted) or a more productive redirect (if not). Both outcomes serve the person better than a polished case built on unexamined desire.
